Before Pearl Harbor, the Japanese had already began imperial expansion in China (1937) and in other territories and islands. The Empire of Japan entered World War II in September 27, 1940, by signing the Tripartite Pact with Germany and Italy, though, it wasn't until the attack on Pearl Harbor on December 7, 1941, that the US entered the conflict. Over the course of seven hours there were coordinated Japanese attacks on the U.S.-held Philippines, Guam and Wake Island and on the British Empire in Borneo, Malaya, Singapore, and Hong Kong. The strategic goals of the offensive were to cripple the U.S. Pacific fleet, capture oil fields in the Dutch East Indies, and maintain their sphere of influence of China, East Asia, and also Korea. It was also to expand the outer reaches of the Japanese Empire to create a formidable defensive perimeter around newly acquired territory.

Japan always knew that they couldn't stand a long term war with the might of the US. They planned to cripple the US pacific fleet and then thought of bringing them to peace settlements. So they started a surprise attack on the Pearl Harbour. Although it involved major casualties, the US didn't settle for peace. They declared war on Japan and entered world war 2 on the side of allies.

Attack on Pearl Harbour

By March 1942, almost all of east and south-east asia fell into Japan's hands. After Hong-Kong fell in December 1941, Malaya and Philippines soon followed. On February 15 1942, Singapore surrendered, causing the largest surrender of British-led military personnel in history. Malaysia and Burma soon followed and Japan almost reached the borders of India. They also started their campeign in China. Large portions of Chinese were taken as prisoners and were used as forced labour.

The tide started turing after US started its offensive in the pacific theatre. Japan was bombed for the first time in the Dolittle raid. In May 1942, Japan failed to defeat the allies in the battle of Coral sea, inspite of their numerical superiority.

Then in June, they lost four of their fleet carriers in the battle of Midway. Even though Japan were numerically superiour, the code breakers turned the tide for the US. Midway proved to be the turning point of the war as the Navy lost its offensive strategic capability and never managed to reconstruct the "'critical mass' of both large numbers of carriers and well-trained air groups".

Japanese aircraft carriers under attack by the US dive bombers in the battle of Midway.

After their navy lost its offensive capabilities, the allies started their offensive and started capturing the Japanese occupied territories one by one. After securing the airfields in Saipan and Guam in the summer of 1944, the United States Army Air Forces undertook an intense strategic bombing campaign, using incendiary bombs, burning Japanese cities in an effort to pulverize Japan's industry and shatter its morale. Regardless, these efforts did not succeed in persuading the Japanese military to surrender. In mid-August 1945, the United States dropped nuclear weapons on the Japanese cities of Hiroshima and Nagasaki. These atomic bombings were the first and only used against another nation in warfare. On August 15 1945, Japan surrendered, bringing an end to the World-War 2.
